# Failures & Lessons

## Following the Crowd

One of Lakshay's earliest mistakes in college was following what everyone else was doing instead of thinking independently.

Like many students, he bought a DSA course because everyone around him said it was important.

After some time, he realised he wasn't learning effectively because he never understood why he was learning it in the first place.

That experience completely changed his mindset.

Today, he always starts with the problem he wants to solve and then learns the skills required for that problem.

It taught him that purpose should come before effort.

---

## Self-Doubt

There was a time when Lakshay wondered whether he was good enough to compete with students from top colleges.

Seeing impressive projects online sometimes made him question his own abilities.

Instead of letting that self-doubt stop him, he kept building projects, participating in hackathons and applying for opportunities.

Winning a national hackathon and securing internships gradually replaced doubt with confidence.

The lesson he took away was simple:

Confidence comes from evidence, not motivation.

---

## Not Focusing on Core Skills Early

If Lakshay could change one decision, he would start focusing on his core technical skills much earlier.

During the beginning of college, he explored many different things, which was useful, but he also realised that spending too much time switching between interests slows long-term growth.

Today, he still believes exploration is important—but only until you discover what genuinely excites you.

After that, depth matters more than variety.

---

## Chasing Perfection

Lakshay naturally likes delivering quality work.

Early on, this sometimes meant spending too much time trying to perfect small details.

Over time he realised that a working product can always be improved after launch.

Now his philosophy is:

Ship something useful.

Learn from feedback.

Keep improving.

Progress beats perfection.

---

## Underestimating Communication

Earlier in his journey, Lakshay believed technical skills alone would determine success.

Hackathons, leadership roles and internships taught him something different.

Explaining ideas clearly, listening to teammates and communicating effectively often have just as much impact as writing good code.

Engineering is a team sport.

---

## Learning to Say No

As opportunities increased, Lakshay realised he couldn't say yes to everything.

Trying to participate in every project, event or technology often meant making slower progress overall.

Today he prefers choosing fewer opportunities and giving them his full attention.

Focused effort consistently produces better results than divided attention.

---

## Failure Isn't Permanent

Lakshay doesn't view failure as the opposite of success.

He sees it as part of the learning process.

Every unsuccessful attempt, rejected application or unfinished idea has contributed something valuable to his growth.

The only real failure, in his opinion, is repeating the same mistake without learning from it.

---

## Biggest Lesson

If Lakshay had to summarize everything his failures have taught him, it would be this:

Don't be afraid of making mistakes.

Be afraid of making the same mistake twice.

Every setback contains information.

The sooner you learn from it, the faster you move forward.

---

## Looking Back

Today, Lakshay is genuinely grateful for many of the mistakes he made.

Without them, he wouldn't have developed the learning philosophy, work ethic and mindset that guide him today.

Success may build confidence, but failures build character.

Both are necessary for growth.
